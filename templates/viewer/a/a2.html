Amnesia

Definition & Overview Types Symptoms Causes Treatment & Care

Pronunciation

["am-NEE-zhuh"]

Definition & Overview

Amnesia refers to a loss of memory, which can be temporary or permanent, and may affect an individualâ€™s ability to recall past events, personal information, or even learned skills. It is not a disease itself but rather a symptom of various conditions, including brain injuries, psychological trauma, or neurological disorders.

Types of Amnesia

  1. Retrograde Amnesia: Difficulty recalling events that occurred before the onset of amnesia.
  2. Anterograde Amnesia: Inability to form new memories after the onset of amnesia.
  3. Transient Global Amnesia (TGA): Sudden, temporary memory loss, often without a clear cause.
  4. Infantile Amnesia: A natural inability to recall early childhood events.

Symptoms

Common symptoms include:

  • Difficulty recalling past events
  • Confusion and disorientation
  • Inability to form new memories
  • Repeated questions or statements
  • Gaps in memory, particularly of significant life events

Causes

Amnesia may result from:

  • Head injuries
  • Stroke or lack of oxygen to the brain
  • Infections affecting the brain
  • Alcohol abuse (Korsakoff syndrome)
  • Psychological trauma

Treatment & Care

Treatment options depend on the cause and type of amnesia:

  • Therapies: Cognitive therapy and memory training exercises
  • Medications: Address underlying causes like infections or vitamin deficiencies
  • Support: Counseling for trauma-related amnesia
  • Assistive Technology: Tools like memory aids or organizers
